Shell Eco-marathon: a lifetime experience.

Jan 25 2026

How can an email change one's life trajectory? It is a question I couldn't imagine myself asking before I visited the Shell Eco-marathon with Dr. Naqaa and my nine other lucky colleagues. Yes, lucky, as we had the chance to have a VIP behind-the-scenes tour with Chemical Engineer Majid Samarah. We passed by multiple teams, asking questions and having a close look at their work and struggles. We had conversations with the Riyadh University, HBKU, and Texas A&M University teams, as well as some international teams from Indonesia and the Philippines. They gave me a lot of insight into their work, the difference between urban cars and prototypes, the engines they use, how they divide the work among themselves, and the challenges they faced.

The team that really stood out to me was the South Korean team. I only asked them for a photo and observed them during the technical inspections. Despite the pressure, stress, and technical issues they faced, they were composed and calm, working coherently as a unit to overcome the obstacles they faced. What I saw was rooted in the idea that teamwork is the essence of success.

After finishing our tour, Engineer Majid invited us to his Majles and served us delicious coffee, where he shared moments of his career and gave us true life lessons. His talk was really inspiring and enjoyable; he was down-to-earth despite his accomplishments, which made me interested in learning more about his experiences. He told us how he got fired from his first job in Jordan, and the reason was an email. He wasn't disrespectful, impolite, or even wrong, but he was so excited that he delivered his ideas in the wrong way, using all caps and a red font, which was indeed misunderstood by his boss.

Nine months after being fired, he got a job at Shell, and 15 years later, he shared his wonderful experience with us, upcoming engineers. His story teaches that no matter how much value and uniqueness your ideas hold, if you deliver them poorly, they become worthless and may harm you. Therefore, being able to express yourself in the right way is equivalent to acquiring knowledge, because success doesn't only depend on your technical intelligence but also on your social and emotional intelligence.

For me, as an electrical engineer, this experience opened my eyes to a path I had limited knowledge of, especially regarding how electric and hybrid cars work. It aroused my interest and curiosity to acquire more knowledge. Talking to engineers and seeing teams work gave me an insight into what my future work will look like if I choose this path, which gave me more options to explore before graduating.

Engineering & Goals.

Jan 25 2026

For this week, we were asked to watch two videos. After I watched them, I learned much more about my career as an Electrical Engineer. I learned that my degree isn't just a series of classes and exams; it is an opportunity to make a change in the community. My electrical engineering coursework provides me with the ability to create and improve systems that transform lives. My goal isn't just to improve technology but to make using technology an easygoing experience, preventing as many drawbacks as possible.

One of the most important issues I would like to address is the issue of energy access, specifically for those without reliable or affordable power — an issue that a lot of people suffer from in my home country, Lebanon. I aim to develop cleaner, smarter, and cheaper energy options, recognizing that good power creates an easier life for all community members who depend on it for their careers and education.

In the years leading up to my graduation, I aim to develop the necessary skills that serve my goal, prioritizing AI skills as AI is becoming the cornerstone of successful workforces. After graduation, I want to work somewhere that blends innovation with social impact — a place where I get the opportunity to put into practice what I have learned. That might be a business that deals with renewable energy, a technology entity that gives back to the community, or even a startup that is determined to make a difference.

My ultimate goal is to have my own company, one that translates my vision into reality. I will be focused, have clear goals, and act in steps to ensure the accomplishment of tasks. To truly get things done, I understand that technical skills alone are not enough; I must also be an effective communicator. I will rely on strong written and oral communication to advocate for my ideas, report my findings, and secure support for my projects from management and stakeholders.

In my journey, I won't be on my own. I will prioritize collaborative work and expanding my social network, where I get to know new people, share experiences, and learn new things. Nonetheless, I will keep a mindset open to learning, where I face my weaknesses and insecurities rather than escaping them, where I lead by example and work day in and day out to turn my dreams into reality.

My Teamwork Identity.

Feb 3 2026

This week, we experienced a unique in-class activity with Dr. Lule that helped us figure out who we are when working in groups. Later, we each took the tendency test individually. I expected to get a result related to leadership, coordination, or being open to all ideas. However, I got the "Questioner", a person who accepts orders or ideas only when they make sense to them. I stick to the decisions I have made, but I question the rules and orders given to me. In other words, I will never do something without a valid reason. You should not expect me to obey orders blindly. Maybe that is why I decided to be independent and pursue an education abroad.

When working in teams, I need clarity, trust, and commitment. The test says Questioners do exhaustive research and check the overall work, which doesn't entirely apply to me. I search for the best ideas and sources, but once the work is divided, I trust my colleagues. My questions help my group maintain the right path. I question bad ideas, inaccurate facts, and things that just don't make sense or serve our goal, preventing us from wasting time. Additionally, I love ideas that stand out and show creativity and effort.

However, having this tendency can affect others because it takes more time to land on the right ideas. My overthinking also backfires on me, sometimes causing stress when the workload is high during exam seasons. For example, last semester in ENG 114, we had to develop a problem question for our research project on a space in Qatar. My teammates suggested many problems, but I kept offering counter-ideas because theirs didn't convince me. We eventually reached the right idea, which helped us win the STEAM showcase competition with our Qur'anic Botanic Garden presentation, but the initial process caused stress and frustration among the team. It felt like I was slowing down the meeting or rejecting everything, but I just wanted to finish the work efficiently and reap the best possible results.

To be a better teammate, I will start explaining why I am asking questions. Instead of just asking questions, which can sound like I am arguing, I will share my reason, which is to ensure we follow the professor's rules, so we don't have to do the work twice. This shows my team that I am trying to help, not just being a difficult person. By respecting the group's goals while still getting the logical answers I need, I can turn my Questioner tendency into a real asset for my group and myself.

Resume Reflection.

Feb 16 2026

When I looked closely at my resume, it felt overwhelming to try and fit all my experiences into a single, clear story. At first, I noticed that my past school achievements, like ranking 16th in the Lebanese Baccalaureate, felt a bit disconnected from my current goal of becoming an electrical engineer. However, adding my strong GPA helps bridge that gap by showing my consistent hard work. What surprised me most was realizing how my non-academic interests — like acting, public speaking, and playing football — actually helped build my professional skills. Seeing them written down proved I already have what it takes to communicate well and work easily within a team.

If I had to rate my resume right now, I would call it a work in progress. It has strong parts, especially regarding group projects. Mentioning my team's win at the STEAM showcase for our Qur'anic Botanic Garden research proves how well I function in a collaborative environment. Still, the resume primarily reflects my high school days and lacks the hands-on technical details that an electrical engineering student needs.

To improve my resume this semester, my main plan is to update it with my recent university work while keeping my true personality and passions. I am pushing to gain more engineering skills on my own, so I am working to finish online courses related to AI and programming. Also, instead of just listing generic skills that anyone can claim to have — like resilience and time management — I want to be specific and make my descriptions as personal as possible to set my resume apart.

Looking further ahead, my main goal is to make my resume stand out by getting real industry experience to match my classes. Joining a UREP research team with seniors from TAMUQ and Lusail University next semester is a big step that will give me valuable experimental research experience. I also plan to look for hands-on technical internships this summer in Qatar or Lebanon. Finally, I want my future resume to clearly show my dedication to giving back to the community. In the end, I want my resume to portray a professional with strong engineering skills, real leadership, and a genuine drive to build sustainable solutions.

Planning the Chaos.

March 15 2026

"What if I didn't travel?" This small question made me want to write this blog. On Feb 22, I decided to surprise my family by traveling to Lebanon to spend spring break with them. I had a great week. I enjoyed quality time with my family, cousins, and friends, all while keeping up with my schoolwork. On March 1, my return flight was scheduled, but suddenly all flights to Qatar were canceled due to the conflicts in the region. I was stuck in Lebanon. I did not know what to feel. Sad because of how the situation turned out, or happy because I was staying longer with my family.

A couple of days passed, and the war started in Lebanon. Beirut and the south were bombed heavily. Things quickly became unsafe and unstable, with power cuts and weak Wi-Fi. This made me very stressed and made it hard to focus on my studies. I felt so anxious, but deep down I knew that everything would pass and that what really matters is that my loved ones and I are safe. I realized that I cannot control what happens around me, but I can control how I react. I set new goals, stopped worrying about the mess, and focused on finding solutions, and pushed myself to keep studying every single day.

Another big challenge was getting used to studying online and at home with my family after studying abroad for six months. After getting used to a routine, everything suddenly changed. So, I put more effort into managing my time and studying. I wanted to make sure I got the results I deserved, even with everything going on. It has not been easy, but it has taught me how resilient I can be. But what if I stayed in Qatar? How would I deal with this situation away from my family? This taught me one thing: God plans our lives for what is best for us, and we should keep that in mind to deal with life's hardships.

As we end this holy month, I hope Lebanon and Qatar stay safe. I hope everything calms down, and I can go back to Qatar to continue the semester and return to my family in better conditions, to the safe and peaceful Lebanon that we miss every day.

The Poster Workshop.

April 5 2026

Before the session with Dr. Lula, I used to view posters in a totally different way. I thought that posters were a visualization of creative projects and always linked them to songs, movies, and games. When it comes to research posters, I would imagine a boring, text-condensed piece of work. However, it turned out that a research poster can be catchy, brief, and easy to look at.

During the session, Dr. Lula taught us key design principles for designing an excellent poster, starting with logically guiding the viewer's eyes. This includes putting the title at the top of the poster, the important stuff like the results in the middle, where people usually look the most, and things like the conclusion at the bottom right, as we usually start looking from the top left to the bottom right when observing any poster.

Color is a crucial aspect of a successful poster. For my team's poster, I will try to suggest colors that reflect the problems we are addressing or the solutions we are suggesting. I will be focusing on 3 to 4 colors split into primary, secondary, and accent colors. These primary and secondary colors should align with each other and contrast with a lighter background color, so the poster becomes as eye-friendly as possible. As we are suggesting sustainable solutions, the light green color would be a great choice. For the font, I don't have any in mind, but any neat and simple, yet professional font will be a good fit.

An important rule that my team and I should keep in mind is the 60:40 rule, where 60% of the poster is visual, and the other 40% is text. Any visual data used should be easy to understand and straight to the point. The target audience is probably my colleagues and Dr. Naqaa. If these techniques are applied properly when making our poster, the audience will have a good grasp on the idea of our research, which is the walkability issue in Education City and how its effects may be mitigated through sustainable solutions.

The visual aspects like the layout design, colors, and aesthetics of the poster, can be easily handled, but the real challenge comes when finding appropriate visual data, like a graph, that is easy to understand and serves the goals of our research.
